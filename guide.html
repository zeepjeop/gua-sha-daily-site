What the research actually says

Early and honest — the science is younger than the tradition.

Microcirculation: measured, four-fold

A laser-Doppler pilot study (Nielsen et al., 2007) found gua sha increased surface blood flow ~400% immediately after treatment, remaining elevated for 25 minutes. Measured on the body with traditional pressure — facial gua sha is far gentler, but works on the same principle.

Contour, tone and elasticity: an encouraging trial

A 2025 randomized controlled trial comparing facial gua sha and roller massage reported measurable improvements in facial contour, muscle tone and skin elasticity over the study period.

The honest caveat

Most gua sha research studied the traditional body technique, and cosmetic claims remain under-researched. That's exactly why Remi promises nothing and photographs everything: your before and after is the only evidence that matters for your face.

Getting it right

DO

  • Use facial oil first — the stone must glide, never drag
  • Keep pressure feather-light; the weight of the stone is almost enough
  • Stroke in one direction, centre of the face outward
  • Go slow — five unhurried minutes beats fifteen rushed ones

DON'T

  • Press hard or "scrape" — facial gua sha is not the vigorous body version
  • Work over active breakouts, broken skin, or sunburn
  • Skip the professional check first if pregnant or managing a skin condition
  • Expect overnight transformation — this is a daily ritual, not a procedure
A note on honesty: gua sha is a self-care practice, not a medical treatment. Individual results vary, effects like the post-massage glow are temporary, and nothing here is medical advice. Move gently, and stop if anything feels uncomfortable.
